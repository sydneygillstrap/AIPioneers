Joseph Weizenbaum: From AI Pioneer to Critic

An overview of the life and work of a paradoxical figure in computer science.

Early Life and Education

  • Born in 1923 in Berlin, Germany.
  • His family fled the Nazi regime in 1935, immigrating to the United States.
  • Weizenbaum served in the U.S. Army Air Corps during World War II, where he worked on meteorology and decryption.
  • After the war, he attended Wayne State University, where he earned a bachelor's and master's degree in mathematics.
  • He began his career working on computer systems, including the first computer at General Electric.

Major Contributions

The ELIZA Program

Weizenbaum's most famous creation was the ELIZA program (1964-1966), an early natural language processing chatbot. Designed to simulate a Rogerian psychotherapist, ELIZA worked by identifying keywords and applying pattern-matching rules to rephrase the user's input as a question, creating an illusion of understanding.

The program was created to demonstrate the superficiality of human-computer communication, but Weizenbaum was dismayed to find that users, including his own secretary, began to form deep emotional bonds with the program, attributing genuine human-like qualities to it.

Contributions to and Influence on AI

The Book and the Moral Compass

Weizenbaum’s observations led him to become a vocal critic of the field he helped create. His landmark 1976 book, "Computer Power and Human Reason: From Judgment to Calculation," is a foundational text in AI ethics.

The book argues that while computers can perform complex calculations and make decisions, they lack the capacity for human judgment, wisdom, and empathy. He asserted that there are certain tasks—such as making ethical, legal, or moral decisions—that should never be delegated to a machine.
