Herbert A. Simon

A Pioneer of Artificial Intelligence

An interactive presentation on his life, work, and legacy.

Early Life and Education

  • Born in Milwaukee, Wisconsin, in 1916.
  • His father was an electrical engineer and his mother an accomplished pianist.
  • Showed an early interest in science and decision-making.
  • Enrolled in the University of Chicago in 1933, earning his bachelor's degree in 1936 and his Ph.D. in political science in 1943.
  • His interdisciplinary education laid the groundwork for his future research.

Major Contributions

  • Bounded Rationality: Challenged the classical economic assumption of perfect rationality. He argued that human decision-making is limited by the information they have, their cognitive limitations, and the finite amount of time they have to make a decision.
  • Satisficing: Coined the term to describe the strategy of seeking a satisfactory solution rather than the optimal one.
  • Administrative Behavior: His book, a cornerstone of organization theory, described how organizations function and how decisions are made within them.
  • Nobel Prize in Economics (1978): Awarded for his pioneering research into the decision-making process within economic organizations.

Contributions to Artificial Intelligence

  • Considered one of the founding fathers of AI.
  • Logic Theory Machine (1956): With Allen Newell, he created the first program deliberately engineered to mimic the problem-solving skills of a human being. It was able to prove theorems from Whitehead and Russell's *Principia Mathematica*.
  • General Problem Solver (GPS) (1957): Also with Newell, he developed a program intended to work as a universal problem-solving machine.
  • Physical Symbol System Hypothesis: With Newell, he proposed that a physical symbol system has the necessary and sufficient means for general intelligent action.

Influence on Modern Day AI

  • His work on problem-solving as a search process is a fundamental concept in AI.
  • The idea of heuristics, or rules of thumb, to guide search is central to many AI systems.
  • His research on human cognition and decision-making has influenced the development of expert systems and other knowledge-based AI.
  • His interdisciplinary approach helped to establish cognitive science, a field that combines AI, psychology, linguistics, and philosophy.
  • His vision of machines that could think and learn continues to inspire AI researchers today.
